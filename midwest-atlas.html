Wealth created through industrial agriculture is exported out of the region, leaving behind depleted soils, nutrient runoff and exploited workers.

Agriculture in the Delta

Prior to 1492, the indigenous nations of the Mississippi Delta were an integral part of trade routes spanning the continent and had class structures, communal land ownerships and large settlements similar to those of the more well-known Aztec and Maya. European colonizers enacted sequential waves of genocide that reduced the number of native people in the region five-fold from 1492 to 1692. What the Spanish and French started, Thomas Jefferson continued and Andrew Jackson finished through the Indian Removal Act of 1830 where ancestral Cherokee, Chickasaw, Creek and Choctaw lands were violently appropriated for cotton, and surviving tribal members were forced to walk to lands in Oklahoma along the infamous and deadly Trail of Tears.
